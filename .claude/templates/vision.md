# Vision Document Template

For documents that define a future state: product visions, platform north stars, team charters, multi-year direction setting.

**Audience**: Leadership and cross-functional stakeholders. They want to understand the direction, be inspired by the ambition, and know what they're committing to. They will challenge anything that feels unrealistic or hand-wavy.

**Typical depth**: 2,000–5,000 words. Vision documents are shorter than strategies — they sell the destination, not the roadmap.

**Human contribution required**: Vision documents need the human's authentic perspective on why this matters and what success looks like. Grounding files help with current-state context, but the vision itself comes from the human, not research.

---

## Core Sections

### Executive Summary
1–2 paragraphs. The vision compressed. A reader who stops here should understand the direction and the ask.

### Why This Matters Now
The catalyst. What changed in the market, technology, or organization that makes this vision timely? Be specific — name the trends, the competitor moves, the internal signals.

### North Star
The destination in one clear statement. Not a mission statement — a concrete description of what the world looks like when this succeeds. The reader should be able to picture it.

### What This Means
Capability definition. Break the vision into 3–5 concrete capabilities or changes. For each: what it is, why it matters, and what it enables. This is where the vision becomes tangible.

### What We Give Back
Human impact. Who benefits and how? Engineers, customers, the business. Be specific about the before/after for real people.

### What Success Looks Like
Measurable outcomes at 6, 12, and 24 months. Not vanity metrics — outcomes that prove the vision is working. Include leading indicators (early signals) and lagging indicators (final outcomes).

### Operating Principles
3–5 principles that guide execution. These are the rules of engagement for the team pursuing this vision. Each principle should resolve a potential debate ("when in doubt, do X not Y").

### Milestones
Major milestones on the path to the north star. Not a detailed roadmap — the strategic waypoints that mark real progress. Each milestone should be independently valuable (not just a stepping stone).

### Leadership Ask
What do you need from leadership? Be explicit: approval, resources, air cover, organizational changes. A vision document that doesn't ask for anything is just an essay.

## Optional Sections

### Principles in Practice
Concrete examples of how the operating principles apply. Scenario: "When faced with X, we choose Y because of Principle N."

### What This Is NOT
Explicit non-goals. What the vision does not cover, promise, or aim for.

### Appendix: Competitive Landscape
Table comparing how competitors or peers approach this space. Strengthens the "why now" argument.

---

## Recommended Drafting Order

For Detailed or Blueprint-depth vision documents, use sectional drafting:

**Round 1** — The pull: `sections:why-this-matters,north-star,what-this-means`
Draft the motivation and the vision. The human reviews to make sure the ambition and direction feel right.

**Round 2** — The substance: `sections:what-we-give-back,what-success-looks-like,operating-principles`
The concrete details. These ground the vision in outcomes and commitments.

**Round 3** — The bookends: `sections:executive-summary,milestones,leadership-ask` + any optional sections
Write the executive summary last. End with a clear ask.

---

## Format Guidance

- **Tone**: Aspirational but grounded. Vision documents can be bolder than strategies, but every bold claim needs either evidence or an explicit acknowledgment that it's a bet.
- **Use tables** for capability comparisons, milestone timelines, and competitive landscapes.
- **Keep diagrams minimal** — a simple before/after or a north-star diagram is sufficient. Vision documents are about clarity, not complexity.
- **Front-load the ambition.** The reader should feel the pull of the vision in the first few paragraphs.
